HW #5: Logical Arguments
Due: 5pm, Friday, October 13

Consider these three premises/facts (adapted from Exercise 5.9 of Gries & Schneider):

  • F0: If Bill goes to class, then if the professor is late Bill learns nothing.
  • F1: If Bill learns nothing and feels good, he does not go to the store.
  • F2: If Bill does not eat, he feels good and does not go to the store.

There are six primitive propositions here:

  • GC: Bill goes to class.
  • LN: Bill learns nothing.
  • PL: The professor is late.
  • GS: Bill goes to the store.
  • FG: Bill feels good.
  • BE: Bill eats.
1. Use the methods from the Gries & Schneider text to show that each of the following conclusions follows from the above premises:

(a) If Bill learns nothing and goes to the store, then he eats.
(b) If the professor is late and Bill feels good and goes to the store, then Bill does not go to class.

Keep in mind that any premise (and the conclusion, for that matter) can be rewritten as any boolean expression to which it is equivalent. (In particular, you might find it helpful to make use of the contrapositive of an implication.)

2. Do Problem 1 again, but this time using resolution (in the proof by contradiction mode, as illustrated in class and on the relevant web page).

3. Using the methods of Gries & Schneider, prove the validity of the following argument.

Louise is lying. If kangaroos are numerous and Louise is lying, then Harriet is lying. If kangaroos are numerous and the dog got outside last night and Harriet is lying, then I am an idiot. If the dog got outside last night, then kangaroos are numerous. If Louise is lying, then the dog got outside last night. Therefore, I am an idiot.

Don't forget that any boolean expression can be rewritten as any expression to which it is equivalent. Also recall that implications have contrapositives.

4. Do Problem 3 again, but this time using resolution.

The remaining problems are with respect to the Island of Knights and Knaves, each of whose inhabitants is either a knight (who always tells the truth) or a knave (who always lies). Among those inhabitants are Bill and Carol. Use b and c, respectively, to stand for the propositions Bill is a knight and Carol is a knight. For each problem, determine the strongest statement you can make with respect to which inhabitants mentioned are knights and which are knaves. If the problem involves the question of whether or not there is gold on the island, determine the strongest statement you can make about the answer to that question.

If the premise of a problem is equivalent to false, indicate that fact.

In all cases, provide a proof in the Gries & Schneider style and heed their advice: If Bill utters a proposition that is formalized by the expression X, this gives rise to the premise b ≡ X, and similarly for Carol.

Theorems that might be useful to you in two of the problems are

(HW5.1) p ≡ p∧q ≡ ¬p∨q
(HW5.2) ((p ≡ q) ⇒ p) ≡ p∨q

(HW5.1) can be proved in two steps using (3.59) and (3.60). If you make use of (HW5.2), provide a proof. In such a proof, (HW5.1) could be useful.

5. Bill said, "If Carol is a knave, I am a knight."
