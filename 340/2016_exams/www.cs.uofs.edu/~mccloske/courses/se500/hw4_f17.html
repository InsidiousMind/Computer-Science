All theorem and exercise numbers refer to the text by Gries and Schneider. In proving a numbered theorem, you may make use of any lower-numbered theorem or metatheorem. (Note that, for example, 3.76a is taken to be a "lower number" than 3.76e.)

Be careful to respect rules of precedence, in particular that ≡ has lower precedence than both ⇒ and ⇐, each of which has lower precedence than both ∧ and ∨.

1. Prove

(3.76e) p ∧ q  ⇒  p ∧ (q ∨ r)

in five different ways:

(a) Prove it using the method of Section 4.1. That is, either transform the antecedant into the consequent or vice versa. (The consequent has "more structure", so it may be better to start with it.) If you transform antecedant into consequent, then in each step the relationship between the two relevant expressions expressions must be either ≡ or ⇒ (implies). If you go in the opposite direction, the relationship in each step must be either ≡ or ⇐ (is consequence of)).

(b) Prove it using the method of assuming the antecedant.

(c) Prove it using the method of proof by case analysis. Use q and ¬q as the two cases.

(d) Prove it using the method of proof by contradiction. (To prove A by contradiction, show ¬A ⇒ false.)

(e) Prove it using the method of proof by contrapositive. (To prove A ⇒ B by contrapositive, show ¬B ⇒ ¬A.)

2. Do Exercise 4.2: Prove the monotonicity of ∧ (4.3),

(p ⇒ q) ⇒ (p ∧ r ⇒ q ∧ r)

3. (Based on Exercises 4.4 and 4.9) Prove

(p ⇒ q) ∧ (r ⇒ s)  ⇒  (p ∨ r ⇒ q ∨ s)
in three different ways.

(a) Prove it using the method of Section 4.1. Here, it is recommended that you transform the antecedant into the consequent. Once again, heed the advice in part (a) of Problem 1.

It is suggested that you use (not necessarily in this order): (3.59) (twice), (3.46) (three times), (3.76d) twice (once to "get rid of" an occurrence of ¬p and once to get rid of an occurrence of ¬r), an absorption theorem (to get rid of an occurrence of q), and DeMorgan (twice).

(b) Prove it by the method of assuming the antecedant. Note that, because the consequent is itself an implication, you can prove it, if you wish, by assuming the antecedant of the consequent. Do not use (4.2) (Monotonicity of ∨) in your proof. Also note that, when you make an assumption, you are, in effect, assuming anything that is equivalent to (or even implied by) your assumption. Hence, for example, by assuming p ⇒ q, you are also assuming, by (3.60), p ∧ q ≡ p.
