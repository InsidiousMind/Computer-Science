Programming Assignments and Homework: 

There will be approximately ten assignments, counting both programming projects and "paper-and-pencil" assignments. Grades on the former will be based upon not only correctness and efficiency, but also on subjective attributes such as quality of documentation, readability, and modularization.

Assignment solutions must identify the student(s) submitting it (e.g., Chris Smith), the course and semester (in this case, CMPS 340, Fall 2016), and the assignment (e.g., HW #2). They should also include remarks that point out any known flaws.

Assignments turned in after the due date are subject to a penalty of seven percent per day. Also, no credit is given for work turned in more than 72 hours beyond the due time.

Students are allowed to collaborate with each other while working on assignments, but such collaboration is to be acknowledged, in writing, within the submitted work. Collaboration should not go so far that it becomes untruthful for a student to claim that submitted work is, for the most part, his or her own (or that of his/her team, in the case of a team project). Under no circumstances is a student to copy the work of another or to allow another student to copy her/his work.

Intended Course Outcomes:

Upon completion of the course, a successful student will have

  • an appreciation for the differences between primary memory and secondary storage as they relate to choices for organizing data (e.g., binary search trees vs. B-trees) and processing data (e.g., internal vs. external sorting)
  • knowledge of alternative approaches for organizing a file of records
  • knowledge of and experience working with at least one data compression algorithm
  • knowledge of and experience working with indexing concepts, including tree-based and hashing-based indexing
  • knowledge of and experience working with relational database concepts, in particular querying using relational algebra
  • an appreciation for basic concepts of information retrieval
